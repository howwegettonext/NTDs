THE THIN LAYER

Neglected Tropical Diseases

Introduction

Discussions about global health usually revolve around malaria, tuberculosis and HIV/AIDS. The “big three”, as they’re known, dominate not only the conversation but also the pool of funding available for public health projects.

But there are other diseases which don’t get the same level of public attention or financial support. These conditions, known collectively as Neglected Tropical Diseases (NTDs), afflict more than a billion people in the world’s poorest countries. In this visual essay we’ll look at what NTDs are, where they’re found, how many people they affect and why they’re so neglected.

What is an NTD?

NTDs are a group of tropical infections which are found most commonly in the poorest communities in Africa, Asia and South America. The group is medically diverse, encompassing parasitic infestations, bacteria and viruses, but what unites them is that they flourish in tropical conditions, and in those of poverty. Most have afflicted humans for centuries, but have gradually disappeared from parts of the world that have improved their living conditions and hygiene. Today, they fester in places where inadequate housing, dirty water and poor sanitation combine to create a environment where they can spread fast and efficiently.

The list of which diseases are recognised as NTDs varies somewhat between different health agencies, and the number has grown over time. But for our purposes, we’ll be using the World Health Organization’s current list of twenty NTDs. Here they are:

Buruli Ulcer

bacteria

A debilitating mycobacterial skin infection causing severe destruction of the skin, bone and soft tissue.

Read more about Buruli Ulcer.

Leprosy

bacteria

A complex disease caused by infection mainly of the skin, peripheral nerves, mucosa of the upper respiratory tract and eyes.

Read more about Leprosy.

Trachoma

bacteria

An infection transmitted through direct contact with infectious eye or nasal discharge, or indirectly through unsafe hygiene practices, which left untreated causes irreversible corneal opacities and blindness.

Read more about Trachoma.

Yaws

bacteria

A chronic bacterial infection affecting mainly the skin and bone, transmitted through direct skin contact with an infected person. Leads to chronic disfigurement and disability.

Read more about Yaws.

Mycetoma

bacteria

A chronic inflammatory skin disease which usually affects the lower limbs. Infection is thought to be caused by a thorn prick or skin damage.

Read more about Mycetoma.

Scabies

mite

A skin infestation by the mite Sarcoptes scabiei, causing severe itchiness and a pimple-like rash. Scabies is most often spread during a relatively long period of direct skin contact with an infected person.

Read more about Scabies.

Chagas Disease

protozoa

A life-threatening illness transmitted through contact with triatomine bugs, ingestion of contaminated food, infected blood transfusions, or organ transplant.

Read more about Chagas Disease.

Sleeping Sickness

protozoa

A parasitic infection spread by the bites of tsetse flies that is almost 100% fatal without prompt diagnosis and treatment to prevent the parasites invading the central nervous system.

Read more about Sleeping Sickness.

Leishmaniasis

protozoa

A disease transmitted through the bites of infected female sandflies that attacks the internal organs and causes face ulcers, disfiguring scars and disability.

Read more about Leishmaniasis.

Snakebites

venom

A snakebite is an injury caused by the bite of a snake, especially a venomous snake. Snakebites were added to the list of NTDs in 2017, after years of criticism of the WHO by activists for not making it a priority.

Read more about Snakebites.

Dengue

virus

A mosquito-borne infection causing flu-like illness that may develop into severe dengue and cause lethal complications.

Read more about Dengue.

Rabies

virus

A preventable viral disease transmitted to humans through the bites of infected dogs that is invariably fatal once symptoms develop.

Read more about Rabies.

Guinea Worm

worm

A nematode infection transmitted exclusively by drinking-water contaminated with parasite-infected water fleas.

Read more about Guinea Worm.

Echinococcosis

worm

An infection caused by the larval stages of tapeworms forming pathogenic cysts in humans and transmitted when ingesting eggs most commonly shed in faeces of dogs and wild animals.

Read more about Echinoccosis.

Trematodiases

worm

An infection acquired by consuming fish, vegetables and crustaceans contaminated with larval parasites.

Read more about Trematodiases.

Lymphatic Filariasis

worm

An infection transmitted by mosquitoes causing abnormal enlargement of limbs and genitals from adult worms inhabiting and reproducing in the lymphatic system.

Read more about Lymphatic Filariasis.

River Blindness

worm

An infection transmitted by the bite of infected blackflies causing severe itching and eye lesions as the adult worm produces larvae and leading to visual impairment and permanent blindness.

Read more about River Blindness.

Snail Fever

worm

A collection of trematode infections transmitted when larval forms released by freshwater snails penetrate human skin during contact with infested water.

Read more about Snail Fever.

STH

worm

A collection of nematode infections transmitted through soil contaminated by human faeces causing anaemia, vitamin A deficiency, stunted growth, malnutrition, intestinal obstruction and impaired development.

Read more about STH.

Taeniasis

worm

An infection caused by adult tapeworms in human intestines; cysticercosis results when humans ingest tapeworm eggs that develop as larvae in tissues.

Read more about Taeniasis.

There are many different reasons for the neglect shown to these diseases, and the biggest is that they only affect the world’s poorest people. “Impoverished and marginalized populations with the NTDs represent the lowest priority markets for U.S. and European pharmaceutical manufacturers,” wrote tropical medicine expert Peter Hotez and a group of other researchers in a 2006 paper. “The NTDs do not occur in the industrialized world or even among the substantial wealthy and middle-classes in developing countries. They are not a significant health risk for foreign travelers or the military. This is in contrast to the more substantial commercial markets for HIV/AIDS, malaria and tuberculosis.”

Other reasons for NTDs’ low profile include social stigma that deters people from seeking medical treatment, long incubation periods with few symptoms (so the connection between a death and a long-held infection is often not realized), and the geographic isolation of the communities where NTDs are most common.

The result of this neglect is that NTD research and treatment programmes receive much lower levels of funding than the big three. Where are NTDs found?

Neglected tropical diseases are not spread evenly throughout the world. As noted above, they primarily affect the world’s poorest countries. NTDs are declining

There’s some good news. Since the World Health Organization began targeting NTDs in 2007, the estimated number of people requiring treatment each year has begun to fall. Numbers have dropped from about two billion in 2010 to a little under 1.6 billion in 2015.

This drop has been driven by a concerted effort by the WHO, by world governments and by the pharmaceutical industry - which has donated large quantities of free medicines that suppress common tropical parasitic and bacterial infections. The key tipping point came in 2012, when the London Declaration on Neglected Tropical Diseases resulted in pharmaceutical companies agreeing to donate two to three billion US$ worth of drugs through 2020, providing close to a billion people per year with access to free treatment. It also included US$785 million for research and development. “The London meeting was a landmark in public health cooperation, setting an ambitious agenda for the next decade,” writes the WHO on its website.

Ambitious goals have been set to keep pushing this progress forwards. The WHO aims to eliminate six of the NTDs (guinea-worm disease, yaws, trachoma, sleeping sickness, leprosy and lymphatic filariasis) as a public health problem by 2020, with a combination of donated medicines and vector control.

The efforts against Guinea-worm disease have been perhaps the most successful—cases have fallen from an estimated 3.5 million in 1986 to just 22 cases in 2015. The number has risen slightly since, to 30 in 2017, mostly due to the discovery that dogs can also carry the disease. But experts believe that the eradication of the disease is now only a matter of time.

But ultimately, solving the world’s NTD problem is really about solving poverty. Sustained programmes for addressing problems around water supply, sanitation, nutrition and housing will have a far bigger long-term effect on the incidence of NTDs than donated pills ever can.

“WHO’s duty is to secure the future of generations to come,” the agency wrote in its 2012 roadmap for the elimination of NTDs. “In spite of several remaining obstacles and challenges, the targets [we have set] are ambitious but achievable.”
